Scientific Work

Steven's work in computer science revolves around "zero trust" otherwise described as "unprivileged computing". His research focuses on a theory he devised known as the "cardinal trust" model. This model allows computer scientists to remove situational awareness when designing computer systems which makes them very difficult, if not impossible, to penetrate.

The key motivation for designing and researching an unprivileged computing model was to deal with increasing threats from persons in a position of trust inside a computer network and the increasing use of computers as digital weapons both by criminals and nation states. His legal experience was crucial in creating a system which truly protects resources and assets in accordance with regulatory requirements.


Key Acheivements

  1. Developed the "cardinal trust" model of unprivileged or "zero trust" computing which provides a way of removing situational awareness to make computer systems incredibly robust.

  2. Created the Atlas.io PAAS software which implements the cardinal trust model on a mass scale. It allows developers to launch payloads without having to be concerned with the security of the underlying platform. Amateur developers and non-programmers are able to use it to acheive security by default.

  3. Various contributions to the open-source community.

Legal Work

Steven has contributed significantly to the state of the law, particularly in regards to the legislation concerning child sexual offences. He has experience of public, criminal and family law. During the sequence of public inquiries related to sexual grooming in the United Kingdom, Steven coordinated evidence in key public inquiries and led a private inquiry into the performance of public bodies. He is also regularly involved in unduly lenient sentence referrals under s.36 Criminal Justice Act 1988 as regards the correctness and scope of judicial power in passing sentence. His family law experience stems directly from working with vulnerable families seeking escape from serious violence.

Having represented hundreds of victims of sexual offences to the Home Affairs Select Committee in 2012 during its inquiry into Localised Grooming and Child Sexual Exploitation, Steven has developed a track record of defending the rights of vulnerable women. He then developed this further by providing representation for those in danger as a result of escalating domestic violence, this through the National Centre for Domestic Violence. This was supplemented by pro bono legal advice and representation in local advice centres in the areas of housing, insolvency, family, consumer and welfare law.

Steven has significant experience of teaching law to professional lawyers and law students in both practice environments and university environments. His teaching efforts are supplemented heavily by his contributions to the law.

Unduly Lenient Sentence Referrals

On a day-to-day basis, I either recruit cases or have them referred to me, in respect of unduly lenient sentences passed in Crown Courts residing in England or Wales. Naturally, the sentences which come to me flow out of extremely serious criminal cases; usually sexual offence cases. When these cases come to me, provided they are potentially unduly lenient, I refer them to the Law Officers (the Attorney-General and the Solicitor-General) under the Unduly Lenient Sentence Scheme (that is the government machinery for s.36 Criminal Justice Act 1988). It takes only one referral under the scheme for the law officers to be required to review a prosecution case.

I draft the Grounds of Appeal and send them to the Attorney-General''s Office. Once my appeal has been considered in respect of a case, one of the Law Officers will write to me, informing me whether he has found the sentence to be unduly lenient or not. If a sentence is unduly lenient in the view of a Law Officer, it can then be referred to the Court of Appeal under s.36 of the Criminal Justice Act 1988. The Court of Appeal then have an opportunity to review the case on submissions of appeal. They will then dismiss the appeal, allow the appeal and change the sentence (based on double-jeopardy principle) or allow the appeal but leave the sentence to stand.
